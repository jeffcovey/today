### Summary of Buddhist thought from Andrew Davis on the PPV discussion boards

In case it helps to further explain where I'm coming from, after many years of digesting the meaning of Buddha's teachings, the central ideas are closely tied to what's being taught here:

- Our inner and outer worlds are constantly changing
- Our mental models of the world are not changing (we resist change and crave stability)
- Therefore we suffer from failing to accept reality
- The central pillar of our mental model is the "I/self/ego"
- Everything else is "other", and we naturally feel alienated / isolated from other
- Some of the things we see are desirable and we develop cravings and addictions to them small and large
- Some of the things we see are undesirable and we develop fear, irritation, and hatred for them
- What we fear most of all is the idea of going out of existence (death)
- In reality, versions of ourselves are coming into and out of existence moment by moment, primarily as a result of our actions
- every action we engage in implies some view of who we are, as well as who/what others are, what we're doing to them, and what they're doing to us
- Our compulsive tendencies drive most of our actions which keep recreating limiting versions of ourselves
- We can train our mind through discipline, meditative absorption, and wisdom to act differently, see ourselves and others differently, and ensure our minds are in harmony with reality.

  ## The "Do" Manifesto

    **Inspired by "[Manifesto of a DOer](https://dolectures.medium.com/manifesto-of-a-doer-dd1baa873f1f)", heavily modified & extended from various inputs and reflections**

    1. The way to attract good luck is to be reliable in a valuable area.The more you repeatedly deliver value, the more people seek you out for that value. Your reputation is a magnet. Once you become known for something, relevant opportunities come to you with no extra work.
    2. "At the higher levels of competitive swimming, something like an inversion of attitude takes place. The very features of the sport that the 'C' swimmer finds unpleasant, the top level swimmer enjoys. What others see as boring‚Äîswimming back and forth over a black line for two hours, say‚Äîthey find peaceful, even meditative, often challenging, or therapeutic. They enjoy hard practices, look forward to difficult competitions, try to set difficult goals. Coming into the 5:30 A.M. practices at Mission Viejo, many of the swimmers were lively, laughing, talking, enjoying themselves, perhaps appreciating the fact that most people would positively hate doing it. It is incorrect to believe that top athletes suffer great sacrifices to achieve their goals. Often, they don‚Äôt see what they do as sacrificial at all. They like it.‚Äù
    3. What are the minority of my actions that drive the majority of my results?
    4. Deadlines serve you best when they are short, hard and (at first glance) impossible. Urgency gets things done.
        - Set Goals that scare you, that you don't know if you can reach using your existing skills and resources. That high bar pressures you to look for new sources of leverage.
    5. Create a habit of always following through. On the big things. On the small things.
    6. Focus on the goal. If something isn‚Äôt pushing the goal forward, it's a distraction. Distractions are the enemy.
    7. Obstacles will come your way, think of them as gifts. They will make you stronger. They will make you more capable. They will define you.
    8. Always ask: "What matters most right now?" That's THE question.
        - "The main thing is to keep the main thing the main thing."
    9. Sprint. Rest. Sprint. Rest. We get more done in bursts followed by rest.
    10. Keep your energy for pushing forward. The past is done. Things out of your control cannot be changed. Energy spent being angry, jealous, or cynical is wasted energy. Don't waste energy, use it.
    11. Say no. And say it often. Protect the zone.
    12. Make a pact with failure early on. Don‚Äôt fear it.
    13. The energy available to get things done is directly proportional to how much it matters to you. Only commit to things that matter.
    14. Find your multipliers: people, teams, tools that can accelerate the change you want to make.
    15. The few influence the many. Find influencers. Be an influencer.
    16. Every minute that you take away from a core activity, or spend distracted by something else, it takes about twenty-three minutes to get back into that flow state of productivity. Those distracted moments represent an enormous opportunity cost.
